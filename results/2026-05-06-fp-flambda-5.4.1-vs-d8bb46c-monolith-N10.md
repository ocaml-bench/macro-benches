**Date:** 2026-05-06
**Host:** monolith (AMD Ryzen 9 9950X, 16 cores / 32 threads, governor=performance, 64 GiB RAM, kernel 6.17)
**Compilers:** `ocaml-5.4.1` (release) vs `ocaml-d8bb46c39bf5fcafb513a8ba18e667d3f8c2600a` (5.5-beta)
**Variants:** 8 — each version × {baseline, -fp, -flambda, -fp-flambda}
**Suite:** 28 macrobenchmarks (27 carried over from the N=3 monolith run + the new `jsoo`; `dune_bootstrap` dropped as a subprocess-bound orchestrator)
**Invocations:** **10 per (benchmark, variant) — 2240 cells total**
**Config:** `running-ng/src/running/config/fp_flambda_macro_5.4.1_vs_d8bb46c.yml` (with `invocations: 10` override)

**Companion:** `2026-05-04-fp-flambda-5.4.1-vs-d8bb46c-monolith.md` (same machine, same compilers, **N=3**). This run is the higher-statistics rerun of that one — all per-cell findings here are reflected against the N=3 numbers, with disagreements called out explicitly. `2026-05-01-fp-flambda-5.4.1-vs-d8bb46c-obelisk.md` is the cross-machine reference (Intel Xeon E5-2667 v2, also N=3).

## TL;DR

This is the rerun the N=3 monolith report flagged as a follow-up — **10 invocations per cell, full data captured for every benchmark including the previously-silent ones** (the runner had a tmpdir-leak bug that lost data for `owl_gc`, `liq_video_frames`, `liq_parse_typecheck`, `ocamlc_self_compile`, `pplacer_testsuite`, and the now-dropped `dune_bootstrap` in the first N=10 attempt; fixed in `running-ng@6ee044b`).

- **The N=3 numbers held up.** Mean |Δ wall-time| across the 216 paired cells (against the published monolith report) is **0.69%**; worst single cell is +4.8% on `liq_video_frames` d8b-flambda. Headline findings — owl_gc, liq_video_frames, alt_ergo_fill, ocamlc_self_compile, cpdf_*, menhir_sysver — all confirmed.
- **Two things did shift materially.** `liq_parse_typecheck` baseline regression dropped from **+10.9% → +5.6%** (the -fp case went the other way: +8.1% → +11.0%, so the original ~10% headline was an artifact of which 3 invocations got sampled). `zarith_pi` baseline shifted from a clean 0.0% to **+2.8%** — small but no longer "flat on monolith"; the cross-machine "Intel-µarch absorbs the page-fault storm worse" finding still holds.
- **New benchmark `jsoo`** (compile the runtime's `ocamlc.byte` to JS) shows a persistent **+15-16% wall regression on d8b across all flag combos**, with RSS dropping ~20%. Profile looks closer to `ocamlc_self_compile` (more allocation, more minor cycles, slightly more majors) than to a pacer-trade story. Worth folding into the existing `ocamlc_self_compile` allocation TODO.
- **Spread was hidden at N=3.** Several benches have per-invocation spread ≥10% at N=10 that the N=3 sampling didn't expose — `eio_fiber_stream` (≤13%), `owl_gc` (≤13%), `coqc_corelib_stress` flambda variants (≤12%), `irmin_mem_rw` d8b-fpfl (16%). The medians are stable; the noise floor is not as quiet as N=3 suggested.

## Findings

### 1. Major-GC pacer change — confirmed at higher statistics

The 1:1 → 2:1 minor:major ratio shift on `owl_gc` and `zarith_pi`
reproduces exactly. Collection counts are deterministic given workload
+ runtime, so this was already nailed down at N=3; N=10 simply confirms
the wall-time consequences are stable rather than artifacts of small-N
sampling.

`owl_gc` baseline is **-36.4%** at N=10 (was -37.1% at N=3); flambda
case is **-26.9%** (was -26.2%). Same shape, magnitudes within 1 pp.
GC overhead drops 64% → 44% on baseline, 36% → 14% with flambda. RSS
drops 17% on baseline, **40% on flambda** (from the 215 MiB the
flambda+5.4 build climbed to). Solid 5.5 release win.

### 2. Pacer regression on promotion-heavy workloads — partly the same shape, with a magnitude correction

`liq_parse_typecheck` is the most interesting reflection point.

| | base | -fp | -flambda | -fp-flambda |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **N=3** | +10.9% ⚠️ | +8.1% ⚠️ | -6.4% ✓ | -4.2% |
| **N=10** | **+5.6% ⚠️** | **+11.0% ⚠️** | -6.3% ✓ | **-6.3% ✓** |

The headline "+10.9% baseline" from the N=3 report turns out to have
been an artifact — the N=10 baseline is +5.6%, just under half. But
the -fp case did the opposite (+8.1% → +11.0%). The flambda flips to
**~6% improvement on both flambda variants** (was -6.4% / -4.2% at
N=3). So:

- The qualitative story holds: pacer is more aggressive on this
  promotion-heavy workload, and flambda's allocation reduction
  reverses the regression.
- The quantitative magnitude on baseline / -fp is more like ±3-4 pp
  than the +10.9% the N=3 number suggested. The pacer regression on
  this bench is real but smaller than originally reported. Worth
  re-checking on obelisk at N=10 too — that machine reported similar
  N=3 numbers (+8.3% baseline) and the same artifact may apply.

GC overhead at N=10: 33% → 39% on baseline (+6 pp), 17% → 12% with
flambda (-5 pp). Same shape as before.

`alt_ergo_fill` is more stable: +8.8% / +5.8% / +8.6% / +6.1% at
N=3 vs **+7.6% / +5.4% / +9.1% / +7.0%** at N=10. All within ±1.5 pp,
all stay flagged. Confirmed.

### 3. The `zarith_pi` cross-machine story — small correction

The N=3 monolith report said zarith_pi was **flat** on baseline
(+0.0%) and the cross-machine "Intel-µarch absorbs the page-fault
storm worse" finding rested on monolith essentially not seeing the
regression. N=10 says baseline is **+2.8%** — small, still well
under obelisk's +17%, but no longer 0. Other flag combos tighten:

| | base | -fp | -flambda | -fp-flambda |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **N=3** | +0.0% | +3.7% | +4.3% | +4.8% |
| **N=10** | **+2.8%** | +3.1% | +3.0% | +5.2% ⚠️ |

The page-fault analysis from §3 of the N=3 report stands —
page-faults grow 3.85× *identically* on obelisk and monolith, the
difference is purely how the memory subsystem absorbs them. What
changes is the framing: monolith **does** see a small, persistent
regression (~3%, with one flag combo crossing the 5% threshold),
just much smaller than obelisk's ~17%. The Zen 5 / DDR5 absorption
is mostly-but-not-completely free.

### 4. New benchmark: `jsoo` — persistent ~15% regression with ocamlc-self-compile shape

`jsoo` (compile the runtime's own `ocamlc.byte` to JS via the
js_of_ocaml `ocaml-5.6` branch + cmdliner 2.1.0) is in this run for
the first time. Headline:

| flag | 5.4 | d8b | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|
| base | 3.16 | 3.67 | **+16.0% ⚠️** |
| -fp | 3.19 | 3.67 | +15.0% ⚠️ |
| -flambda | 2.86 | 3.29 | +15.0% ⚠️ |
| -fp-flambda | 2.91 | 3.33 | +14.6% ⚠️ |

The collection / allocation profile:

| metric (5.4 → d8b base) | 5.4 | d8b | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|
| total minor-heap alloc | 4.52 GB | 4.69 GB | +3.8% |
| minor collections | 2260 | 2348 | +3.9% |
| major collections | 28 | 33 | +17.9% |
| GC overhead | 28.6% | 34.2% | +5.6 pp |
| max_rss | 341 MiB | 273 MiB | **-19.8%** |

This isn't a clean pacer-trade (allocation grew, GC overhead grew,
RSS shrank, wall regressed) — it looks closer to the `ocamlc_self_compile`
profile (TODO entry filed 2026-05-06) than to a `cpdf_merge`-style
pacer story. Compiler-internals workload allocating differently in
d8b: probably worth folding into the same investigation since both
benches stress overlapping parts of the compiler stack (typing,
Hashtbl, Marshal).

### 5. Smaller persistent regressions — confirmed

Same set as the N=3 report, magnitudes within ±1 pp:

- `cpdf_merge` +5.6% (was +5.4%)
- `cpdf_scale` +4.6% (was +3.2%)
- `cpdf_squeeze` +3.9% (was +2.7%)
- `cpdf_blacktext` +2.6% (was +2.9%)
- `irmin_mem_rw` +4.1% (was +3.3%)
- `menhir_sysver` +6.4% (was +5.9%)
- `ocamlc_self_compile` +8.8% (was +8.2%)

All have ≥30% GC overhead and shrinking heaps under d8b. Same "more
aggressive pacer" story as before, with the caveat from
ocamlc_self_compile that **at least for that one** the mechanism is
actually allocation growth rather than pacer pressure (see TODO).

### 6. `liq_video_frames` — RSS win exact, wall slightly different per flag

The -14% RSS finding reproduces **bit-for-bit across all 4 flag
combos**: 602 MiB → 517-518 MiB at every variant. Wall-time deltas
moved a couple of points per variant:

| | base | -fp | -flambda | -fp-flambda |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **N=3** | +19.5% | +16.8% | +13.0% | +9.8% |
| **N=10** | +17.7% | +16.2% | **+15.9%** | +10.6% |

Notable: the -flambda case grew (was +13.0%, now +15.9%) — this is
the largest single-flag-combo shift among headline cells. Doesn't
change the conclusion (RSS win, wall cost shrinks with flambda) but
worth noting that flambda doesn't help the wall regression on this
bench as much as N=3 suggested.

### 7. `pplacer_testsuite` — no longer "borderline noise"

## Methodology notes

- **Median over mean** for all headline numbers, matching the N=3
  reports.
- **Spread** (max − min)/median is now visible at N=10. Mean wall-time
  spread across the 224 cells is **2.0%**; median is 1.0%. Worst:
  `irmin_mem_rw` d8b-fp-flambda at 16.0% — driven by a single 4.40s
  outlier in a series otherwise around 3.78s. Other ≥10% cells are
  `eio_fiber_stream` (4 variants), `owl_gc` (3 variants),
  `coqc_corelib_stress` -flambda. Eio is a known fiber-scheduler
  noise source; owl is finalisation-noisy at the ring buffer's
  small-N regime; coqc's spread is consistent with the I/O-bound
  profile of that benchmark. None of these affect the medians used
  in the tables above; they do mean that any cell-level claim within
  ±5% should be treated with the spread in mind.
- **No formal statistical test.** N=10 is enough for medians to be
  stable but not enough for proper significance testing (Le Boudec
  2010 / Chen 2012 criteria want ~30 per cell). For now, ≥5% is the
  flagging threshold and 2-5% is the "interesting but inconclusive"
  band.
- **Runner data integrity.** This rerun follows
  `running-ng@6ee044b` which fixes a tmpdir-leak that caused the
  first N=10 attempt (2026-05-04) to silently lose data on
  GC-heavy benches because /tmp filled up and the runtime_events
  ring couldn't grow. All 2240 cells in this run captured cleanly.
- **`liq_parse_typecheck` invocation-level inspection** would be
  useful to understand the base/-fp asymmetry — both run the same
  workload but the N=10 medians say one regresses 5.6% and the
  other 11.0%. Could be a real frame-pointer interaction with the
  pacer, or just two different draws of a noisy distribution.
